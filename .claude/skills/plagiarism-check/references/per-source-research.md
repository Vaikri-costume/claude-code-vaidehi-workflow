# Per-Source Brief — Research Essay Additions

Appended to per-source.md for research essays. Modifies checks 1, 2, and 3 for the research context.

---

## Check 1 addition — Technical terminology calibration

Domain-specific terms that appear in both the source and the draft do not constitute overlap if they are the field's standard vocabulary — the only possible terms for the concept.

To identify field-standard terms: look at the source's own usage. Does the source treat the term as established (no definition, no citation for the term itself)? If yes, it is probably field-standard. If the source defines or attributes the term, it is probably specific to that source or author.

Flag only non-standard multi-word phrases — combinations specific to this source's formulation, not the field at large.

---

## Check 2 addition — Synthesis standard for research essays

In research essays, synthesis requires more than using a source to support a claim. The writer must show how the source fits into a larger scholarly conversation — where it agrees, complicates, or contradicts other sources they have cited. A paragraph that uses one source without situating it in relation to other scholarship may still be summary, not synthesis, even if the claim is the writer's.

---

## Check 3 addition — Theoretical framework attribution

In postcolonial studies, film studies, cultural studies, and related fields, attributing a theoretical framework to its originator is required even for widely used or seemingly obvious concepts.

Examples that require attribution even when familiar:
- The male gaze — attributed to Mulvey even in passing references
- Othering — attributed to Said or Spivak depending on specific usage
- Performativity — attributed to Butler
- The subaltern — attributed to Spivak

Flag the ABSENCE of such attribution even when the framework seems like common knowledge in the field. "It is well known that..." does not constitute attribution.
